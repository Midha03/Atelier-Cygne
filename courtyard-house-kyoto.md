The house is easy to miss from the street — a long, windowless timber wall with a single door. It is only once you're inside that the plan reveals itself: every room faces inward, toward a courtyard barely five metres across.

This inversion, common in traditional Kyoto townhouses, solves a problem that most Western houses never bother to ask: how do you get privacy and daylight from the same design move? Here, the courtyard does both jobs at once, admitting sky while keeping the street at a careful distance.

## What the courtyard teaches

Living around a courtyard changes the rhythm of a day. Meals happen with a view of a fixed piece of garden rather than a changing street scene. Rain becomes something to watch rather than something to avoid. The house, in a sense, has weather built into its floor plan.

It's a model worth borrowing from even at a much smaller scale — a single interior window onto a light well, or a room organised around one framed view, can do a modest version of the same thing.
