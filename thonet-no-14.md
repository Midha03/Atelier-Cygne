Michael Thonet's No. 14 chair is, by most counts, the best-selling piece of furniture ever made — bent beechwood, six pieces, ten screws, a design so efficient it could be shipped flat and assembled anywhere.

What gets lost in the history of its manufacturing is how strange it must have looked in 1859: a chair with almost no straight lines, made possible only by steaming wood until it would bend like something alive.

## Why it still matters

Most furniture ages by going out of fashion. The No. 14 has avoided this simply by never fully belonging to a single moment — it has sat in Parisian cafés, Bauhaus classrooms, and minimalist apartments without ever looking out of place in any of them.

That is the real lesson for anyone furnishing a room today: the objects that last are rarely the ones designed to make a statement. They are the ones designed to solve a problem so completely that the solution becomes invisible.
